Chapters 1-15 Study Guide: Summaries, Characters, and Vocabulary

Chapter 1 Summary

Jonas, an eleven-year-old boy, lives in a seemingly perfect community where everything is controlled and organized. He feels anxious about the upcoming December Ceremony, where he will receive his life Assignment. The story begins when Jonas sees a mysterious aircraft flying over the community, which is unusual and frightening for everyone. Later, Jonas talks with his family during their nightly ritual of sharing feelings. His father, a Nurturer who cares for babies, mentions a newchild named Gabriel who isn't developing well. Jonas's younger sister Lily shares her anger about a visiting group of Sevens who didn't follow the rules. Jonas describes his feeling as "apprehensive" about the Ceremony, though he's not scaredâ€”just uncertain about his future.

Key Points

  • The community is highly controlled with strict rules
  • Jonas is nervous about the December Ceremony and his Assignment
  • Families share their feelings every evening
  • Jonas's father works as a Nurturer for babies
  • Jonas's feeling is **apprehensive** (nervous but not scared)

Characters

Jonas
The eleven-year-old protagonist who is thoughtful and sensitive. He's waiting to receive his Assignment at the December Ceremony.
Father
Jonas's father, a gentle Nurturer who takes care of newchildren in the community.
Mother
Jonas's mother who works at the Department of Justice.
Lily
Jonas's younger sister who is seven years old. She's energetic and speaks her mind.

Important Vocabulary

Apprehensive
Feeling worried or nervous about something that might happen in the future.
Assignment
The job or role that each person receives at age twelve in the community.
Nurturer
A person whose job is to take care of babies and young children.
Newchild
A baby or very young child in the community.
Precision of Language
The rule of using exactly the right words to express feelings and ideas clearly.

Chapter 2 Summary

Jonas remembers the Ceremony from when Lily became a Seven and received her front-button jacket. He explains how each age group receives something new at the December Ceremonyâ€”younger children get new clothing items, while older ones gain more independence. Jonas recalls a significant event from years ago when a girl named Katharine was "released" from the community after being given the wrong Assignment. His parents help him understand that the Committee of Elders carefully observes everyone to choose the right Assignment. They explain that very rarely, someone can apply for release, but this almost never happens. Jonas's mother reassures him that the Elders never make mistakes and that he shouldn't worry. His father mentions again that he's concerned about Gabriel, the newchild who isn't sleeping well at night.

Key Points

  • Each age group receives specific items at the December Ceremony
  • The **Committee of Elders** observes children carefully before choosing Assignments
  • Release from the community can happen but is very rare
  • The concept of release is introduced but not fully explained
  • Jonas's father breaks a rule by looking at the Naming list and secretly calling the newchild **Gabriel** (or Gabe).

Characters

Committee of Elders
The group of leaders who observe children and decide their Assignments for the future.
Gabriel (Gabe)
A newchild who isn't developing normally and has trouble sleeping at night. Jonas's father starts calling him by his assigned name secretly.
Katharine
A girl from the past who was given the wrong Assignment and later released from the community.

Important Vocabulary

Ceremony
An important formal event where children receive new items or privileges based on their age.
Release
When someone leaves the community permanently (the true meaning is unclear at this point).
Committee of Elders
The group of wise leaders who make important decisions for the community.
Independence
The ability to do things on your own without help from others.
Observe
To watch carefully to learn about someone's abilities and personality.

Chapter 3 Summary

Jonas watches his father bring Gabriel home from the Nurturing Center. He notices something unusual about the newchildâ€”Gabriel has **pale eyes** like Jonas, which is very rare in the community where most people have dark eyes. While playing catch with Asher, Jonas takes an apple home because he notices something strange about it, though he can't explain what. He tossed it, and for an instant, it seemed to **change** in mid-air. Later, he hears an announcement reminding the community that taking food home is against the rules. Jonas also remembers an incident where he saw something change about the faces of people in the crowd, similar to what happened with the apple. When Jonas sees Larissa, an old woman, at the House of the Old, Fiona tells him that she'll soon be released in a celebration. Jonas is curious about what happens during release ceremonies but doesn't get complete answers.

Key Points

  • Gabriel and Jonas both have unusual **pale eyes**
  • Jonas notices something strange about an apple but can't explain it (a glimpse of the color red)
  • The community has strict rules about taking food home
  • Jonas is beginning to see things differently from others
  • The mystery of release continues to grow

Chapter 4 Summary

Jonas rides his bicycle to volunteer at the House of the Old with his friend Fiona. He enjoys spending time there, especially with the elderly residents. During his volunteer shift, Jonas helps bathe an old woman named Larissa. While bathing her, Larissa tells Jonas about **Roberto's release ceremony** that happened earlier that morning. She describes it as a wonderful celebration where Roberto's whole life was told through speeches and everyone was happy. At the end, Roberto walked through a special door and was released. Larissa doesn't know what's actually behind that door or where people go when they're releasedâ€”no one ever comes back to tell about it. Jonas finds this strange but Larissa seems content and doesn't question it. The conversation shows how the community celebrates the elderly, but the mystery of release becomes even more puzzling.

Key Points

  • Jonas volunteers at the House of the Old and enjoys it
  • Release ceremonies for the elderly are celebrations of their lives
  • No one knows what actually happens during release
  • People in the community don't question what they don't know
  • The community shows respect and care for the elderly

Characters

Chapter 5 Summary

Jonas wakes up from a confusing dream about his friend Fiona. In the dream, he wanted her to get into a bathtub, and he felt strange feelings that he couldn't understand. During the morning ritual of sharing dreams, Jonas tells his family about it. His parents explain that he's experiencing "**Stirrings**"â€”the first feelings of physical attraction that happen during adolescence. His mother gives him a **pill** that he must take every morning to stop these feelings. She explains that everyone in the community takes pills to control the Stirrings once they begin. Jonas feels disappointed because the pleasant feelings from his dream will disappear, but he understands this is how things work in his community. His father also shares that Gabriel will be brought to their home at night to see if extra care can help the newchild develop better.

Key Points

  • Jonas experiences his first **Stirrings** (feelings of attraction)
  • The community controls feelings through **daily pills**
  • Everyone must take pills once Stirrings begin
  • Jonas feels sad about losing the pleasant feelings
  • Gabriel will come to Jonas's home at night for extra care

Characters

Important Vocabulary

Chapter 7 Summary

The Chief Elder pauses the ceremony after skipping Jonas's number, Nineteen, causing extreme tension and discomfort in the Auditorium. Jonas feels a crushing weight of humiliation and fear, believing he has done something wrong to be excluded from his fate. He dares not look at his parents for fear of seeing their shame. The Chief Elder finally breaks the silence, smiling benignly, and apologizes to the community and to Jonas for the anxiety she has caused. She instructs Jonas to come forward to the stage.

Key Points

  • Jonas is sitting in his original birth order spot: **Nineteen**.
  • The Chief Elder announces **Twenty** (Pierre), confirming Jonas has been skipped.
  • The entire community experiences palpable **discomfort** and silence.
  • Jonas is overwhelmed by **humiliation** and terror at the thought of his failure.
  • The Chief Elder makes a formal **apology** for causing anguish.

Chapter 8 Summary

The Chief Elder announces that Jonas has not been assigned, but **selected**, as the communityâ€™s next **Receiver of Memory**. This position is highly honored and rare. The Chief Elder outlines the necessary attributes for the Receiver: Intelligence, Integrity, Courage (the training involves physical pain), Wisdom, and finally, the mysterious **Capacity to See Beyond**. Jonas is introduced to the current Receiver, a bearded man with pale eyes who is watching him intently. The Chief Elder also solemnly references the failure of the previous selection ten years ago, an event that caused the community immense suffering.

Key Points

  • Jonas is **selected** to be the new **Receiver of Memory**.
  • The role is deeply honored, but the Receiver must live and train **alone**.
  • The selection requires five core attributes: Intelligence, Integrity, Courage, Wisdom, and the **Capacity to See Beyond**.
  • The training will involve **pain** of a magnitude the community cannot comprehend.
  • Jonas has a fleeting experience of "seeing beyond" as he looks at the crowd.

Chapter 9 Summary

Jonas immediately feels separated from his friends, who are hesitant and treat him differently. That evening, he opens his instructions folder and finds only one sheet of paper with eight rules. The rules are startling, including being **exempted from rules governing rudeness** and the **prohibition of dream-telling and medication**. The final rule is the most terrifying: **You may lie**. Jonas is shocked, realizing that since he is allowed to lie, other adults in the community might also have this rule, meaning he can never trust anyone's words, not even his parents'. He asks his parents about the failed Receiver, a female whose name is *Not-to-Be-Spoken*, a phrase indicating the highest disgrace.

Key Points

  • Jonas feels immediate **apartness** from his peers and family.
  • He receives only a single sheet of paper with **eight rules**.
  • Rules include the freedom to ask rude questions and the restriction of most medication.
  • He is forbidden to discuss his training or engage in dream-telling.
  • The final rule: **You may lie**â€”causes Jonas to doubt all adults.
  • The previous failed Receiver was a female whose name is **Not-to-Be-Spoken**.

Chapter 10 Summary

Jonas reports to the Annex, a small wing behind the House of the Old. He notices the door is locked, a huge transgression of community rules. The Attendant respectfully stands for him. Inside, the training room is luxurious and, most notably, filled with **thousands of books**â€”more than Jonas ever knew existed. Jonas meets the current Receiver, who instructs Jonas to call him **The Giver**. The Giver explains that his job is to transmit all the **memories of the whole world**'s past to Jonas, spanning back generations before the community chose "Sameness." He likens the burden of holding memories to pushing a sled uphill in deep snow.

Key Points

  • The Annex has a **locked door** for privacy, a rare sight in the community.
  • The Giver's dwelling is filled with thousands of books, representing centuries of lost knowledge.
  • The Giver must transmit not just his past, but the **memories of the entire world**.
  • These memories are the source of the Giver's **wisdom** and are painful to hold.
  • The Giver gives Jonas permission to ask any question.

He experiences the **exhilarating** sensation of speed and cold. When the memory ends, The Giver explains that snow, hills, and sleds were eliminated by the community's choice of **Sameness**â€”specifically through **Climate Control**â€”to ensure efficiency and easy transport.   • The Giver explains that transferring a memory slightly lessens his own burden.

Chapter 12 Summary

Jonas easily lies to his parents about having no dreams. The next day, while waiting for Fiona, he sees her hair change in a strange, fleeting way, similar to the apple incident. He tells The Giver about the changing apple, the faces at the Ceremony, and Fiona's hair. The Giver correctly identifies this as Jonas starting to see the color **red**. He explains that the community chose **Sameness**, which meant sacrificing all colors along with other differences. Because color is no longer needed, the community has evolved to suppress the ability to see it, but Jonas's Capacity to See Beyond is breaking through. The Giver promises that Jonas will see all colors when he receives all the memories.

Key Points

  • Jonas's Capacity to See Beyond manifests as seeing the color **red**.
  • Fiona's red hair is the strongest example of color in the community.
  • The community **relinquished color** along with other differences to achieve Sameness.
  • Jonas can only see flashes of color initially; The Giver sees all colors all the time.
  • The Giver gives Jonas the memory of a **rainbow** to help him understand the concept of color.

Chapter 13 Summary

Jonas is angry that the community gave up colors and choice. He passionately argues with The Giver that if everything is the same, there are no choices, which he finds unfair. The Giver counters by asking Jonas to imagine if people chose their own Assignments or spouses and made mistakes, leading to unsafe outcomes. Jonas concedes that the lack of choice ensures safety and order, but is still frustrated. He attempts to transmit the color red and memories (like one about elephant grief) to Asher and his family but is unsuccessful and accidentally causes Lily pain, realizing he cannot change things for them. The Giver tells Jonas that he once had a spouse, but she now lives with the **Childless Adults**, confirming Jonas's eventual isolation.

Key Points

  • Jonas recognizes that the lack of color and choice is a trade-off for **safety** and **order**.
  • He is unsuccessful in trying to transmit a memory to Asher and Lily.
  • The memory of the elephants shows Jonas the concepts of **grief** and **love** in the past.
  • The Giver reveals that he had a spouse, but due to his role, he had to live in isolation, and Jonas will face the same loneliness.
  • Jonas is increasingly frustrated and **angry** at the sameness of the community.

Characters

The Giver

Chapter 14 Summary

Jonas asks to take on some of The Giver's pain. The Giver gives him a second sled ride memory, but this time, the hill is icy. Jonas falls, breaks his leg, and experiences **excruciating pain** and isolation. He screams for relief-of-pain medication, but The Giver is forced to refuse. The Giver explains that these painful memories give him **wisdom**, citing an instance when the Elders considered increasing the population. The Giver used the memory of **hunger** and **warfare** to advise them against it. That night, Jonas's father reports that newchild Gabriel is still fretful and may be **released**. When Gabriel wakes up, Jonas tries to comfort him and accidentally discovers that he can **transmit a memory** of a pleasant sail to the newchild.

Key Points

  • Jonas receives a memory of severe physical pain: a **fractured leg** on an icy hill.
  • The Giver uses painful memories to gain **wisdom** for advising the Elders.
  • The memory of **hunger and war** was used to advise against increasing the population.
  • Jonas's father threatens to **release Gabriel** because he is failing to thrive.
  • Jonas discovers his hidden power to **transmit** memories to Gabriel.

Chapter 15 Summary

Jonas enters the Annex and finds The Giver in so much pain that he immediately begs Jonas to take some of it. Jonas lies down and is plunged into a gruesome memory of **warfare**. He sees a chaotic, foul-smelling battlefield filled with dying men and animals. He is a young soldier, desperately trying to give water to a mortally wounded, blond-haired boy next to him. Jonas witnesses the boy die and hears the horrific sounds of battle, experiencing overwhelming emotional and physical trauma. When the memory ends, Jonas is deeply disturbed and the experience of true suffering leaves The Giver distraught and full of regret.

Key Points

  • The Giver is in immense pain and desperately needs Jonas's help to take the memory.
  • Jonas receives a memory of a devastating **battlefield (war)**.
  • He witnesses death, pain, and the vivid colors of **blood** and carnage.
  • Jonas feels immobilized by the pain and the scene's overwhelming suffering.
  • The Giver apologizes for inflicting such a terrible memory, demonstrating his deep empathy.
